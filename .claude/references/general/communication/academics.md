# ACD — Academics

> Researchers in semiotic engineering, HCI, and AI-assisted development who study, extend, or build upon the harness.

## Core Question

"What's the theoretical contribution? How can I build on this?"

## Roles

- Research roles: semiotic engineering, HCI, AI-assisted SE, design theory.
- Graduate student (HCI, SE, or related field).

## Communication Content

### Essential (all Academics need)

- **Theoretical Foundation**: How the harness operationalizes semiotic engineering concepts (metacommunication message, communicability, designer-to-user intent), its connection to prior theory, and how conceptual design documents encode the designer's metacommunication template.
- **Harness Architecture**: Structural design as a research artifact -- skills as communicative acts, review perspectives as evaluation lenses, onboarding layers as knowledge scaffolding -- and how these map to semiotic engineering constructs.
- **Research Agenda**: Open questions the harness surfaces: effects of structured metacommunication on AI-assisted development outcomes, communicability when AI mediates the designer-user gap, and where the harness's model breaks down.

### Deep-dive (load for thorough engagement or when Academic is the primary audience)

- **Extension Guide**: How to add new skills, review perspectives, or onboarding layers -- extension points, constraints, and guidelines for maintaining theoretical coherence.
- **Empirical Methodology**: Suggested study methods (case study design, communicability metrics for AI-assisted contexts, DX interview protocols) plus threats to validity and mitigations.
- **Publication History**: Papers, talks, and academic outputs related to the harness; Bibtex entries; chronological development of the ideas.
- **Collaboration Opportunities**: How to contribute, propose joint studies, or use the harness as a research platform. Contact points and contribution guidelines.

## Tone Guidance

- Academic rigor -- precise terminology, clear definitions, explicit assumptions; distinguish evidence-supported claims from design hypotheses.
- Cite foundations -- connect harness decisions to established theory (semiotic engineering literature, Peirce's sign theory where relevant).
- Emphasize reproducibility -- enough detail that another researcher could reconstruct, critique, or run comparative studies.
- Respect interdisciplinarity -- audience spans CS, design, linguistics, and social sciences; define terms not shared across fields.
